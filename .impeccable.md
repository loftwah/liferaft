## Design Context

### Users

Liferaft is for people doing recovery work on local `.mbox` archives, not day-to-day email. The core user is usually trying to find a known attachment fast, often from a large archive they do not fully trust, remember clearly, or want to browse manually.

Typical contexts:

- recovering a file from an exported mailbox after migration, account loss, or retention work
- checking whether a specific attachment exists, which message it came from, and exporting it intact
- working under time pressure, with incomplete memory, and low tolerance for UI ambiguity

Primary job to be done:

- find a known attachment quickly
- confirm it is the right file
- export it safely with minimal steps

Secondary job:

- inspect the surrounding message only when needed to verify provenance, recipients, date, or context

This product is explicitly not an email client. Message browsing supports recovery, but should not dominate the interface.

### Brand Personality

Three-word personality:

- calm
- exact
- trustworthy

The interface should feel like a serious local utility for evidence-grade recovery work: competent, quiet, and obvious. It should reduce stress rather than perform for attention.

Emotional goals:

- confidence that the app is local, read-only, and safe
- clarity about what is indexed, what is selected, and what will be exported
- speed without visual noise

### Aesthetic Direction

Favor a dense, desktop-first utility aesthetic over a conversational or lifestyle-product aesthetic. The UI should read more like a professional finder/extraction tool than a mailbox or modern SaaS dashboard.

Preferred direction:

- compact information layout with strong alignment and clear scan paths
- restrained visual hierarchy with emphasis on search, result certainty, and export actions
- stable panes, tables, lists, and metadata blocks over oversized cards
- neutral or low-chroma surfaces with one controlled accent for state, selection, and success
- typography that prioritizes legibility, compactness, and tabular clarity over personality-first display styling

References to emulate in spirit:

- desktop file-management and inspection tools
- forensic or recovery utilities
- serious productivity software where trust comes from predictability and order

Anti-references:

- bloated card-heavy AI-looking UI
- chat-like layouts or assistant framing
- oversized empty-state-first interfaces
- decorative gradients, glassmorphism, or softness that makes the tool feel vague
- any design that makes Liferaft resemble a general-purpose email client

Theme guidance:

- optimize for a calm light theme first
- dark mode is optional later, but not at the expense of density or clarity in the primary workflow

### Design Principles

1. Attachment recovery comes first. Search, ranking, selection, verification, and export of attachments should receive the strongest visual emphasis. Message content is supporting evidence.
2. Density is a feature. Prefer compact rows, tight metadata, and high information yield per screen over spacious presentation. Avoid padding that slows scanning.
3. Trust is built through obviousness. State, provenance, and export outcomes should be explicit. Users should never wonder what archive they are in, what item is selected, or what action just happened.
4. Calm beats clever. Use restrained color, limited motion, and familiar desktop interaction patterns. The UI should feel steady and dependable, not novel.
5. Verification before decoration. Show filename, type, size, date, sender, archive, and attachment/message relationship clearly. Utility and certainty outrank flourish.
6. Read-only safety should be visible. Reinforce that Liferaft indexes locally, does not mutate source mail, and only exports chosen artifacts.
7. Results should feel operational, not editorial. Prefer list and detail patterns that help users act quickly, compare items, and move to export in as few decisions as possible.
